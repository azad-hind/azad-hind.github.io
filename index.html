Intel File 001 · The Civilian Network
The Kirani Network
Long before the first shot was fired, INA established a clandestine civilian intelligence network — the Kirani Network — woven through Malaya, Burma, and Thailand. Shopkeepers, doctors, teachers, and lawyers fed information through an ingenious chain of couriers. Their identities remain classified to this day.

Intel File 002 · The Constitution Before the Constitution
Before the Assembly
Two years before India's Constituent Assembly convened, the Azad Hind Government drafted a 47-page constitutional framework — universal adult franchise, abolition of untouchability, equal rights for women. B.R. Ambedkar reportedly called it "more progressive than anything Congress had proposed." It sits in the National Archives, largely unread.
03
Intel File 003 · The First Free India
Andaman Islands, 1943
On 30 December 1943, Netaji raised the tricolour over the Andaman Islands — 4 years before independence. He renamed them "Shaheed Dweep" and "Swaraj Dweep." For 18 months, a fully Indian-administered government functioned with its own laws, postal stamps, and governance. One paragraph in most textbooks. A world-changing event in reality.

Intel File 004 · Nine Nations
The Recognised Government
The Azad Hind Government maintained active embassies in Tokyo, Berlin, Rome, Bangkok, Nanking, Shanghai, Manchukuo, Croatia, and the Philippines. Nine nations formally recognised it. INA even signed treaties awarding Indian sovereignty over the Andamans. The British dismissed them as "rebels." History remembers them as patriots.

Intel File 005 · The 120km Moment
The Tipping Point at Dimapur
INA troops came within 120km of Dimapur — a tipping point that would have collapsed British Northeast India. They were stopped not by British rifles alone, but by monsoon, disease, and severed supply lines. They never stopped believing in a free India.

Azad Hind Radio
Operating from Singapore and Berlin, INA's radio network broadcast in 12 Indian languages — reaching Indian soldiers serving with British forces across the world. Strategic Impact Report · February 1946

The Mutiny That
Ended an Empire

"78 ships. 20,000 sailors. One flag — not the Union Jack. The Royal Indian Navy mutiny of 1946 was not a footnote. It was the last page."

On 18 February 1946, ratings aboard HMIS Talwar in Bombay went on strike over food, racial discrimination, and solidarity with the INA prisoners on trial. Within 72 hours, 78 ships of the Royal Indian Navy had hoisted the INA flag simultaneously. The mutiny spread to shore establishments in Bombay, Karachi, Calcutta, and Madras.

The British Prime Minister Clement Attlee later admitted privately that the INA trials had been a catastrophic miscalculation — they turned the defendants into heroes and put the entire concept of Indian loyalty to the Crown on trial instead.

"The INA trials convinced every Indian in uniform that their real loyalty lay with India. When the sailors mutinied, we knew the game was over. No empire survives when its own soldiers refuse to defend it."
— Clement Attlee, British Prime Minister 1945–61
